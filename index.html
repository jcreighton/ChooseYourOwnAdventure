Part 1: ECMA

You are an intergalactic engineer with a perplexing mystery to solve. Your ship, the SSH Code, has received a distress signal from a nearby planet, ECMA.
“Something is wrong. Our systems are in disarray. Help.”
You landed on the planet a few seconds ago. Before you can so much as look around...
“THANK GOODNESS YOU ARE HERE!”, a voice yells. “It’s been days and the alerts keep growing and we aren’t sure why and it’s not stopping. And I didn’t know if anyone would see the distress signal.”
The ECMIAN stares, wide-eyed and hopeful, at you.
“Oh, I should introduce myself. I’m W. Orld, Chief Operating Officer of ECMA. Usually I wouldn’t need to send out a distress signal but our engineers are at JSConf Earth and the reports keep coming and I… I need help.”
“I’m happy to help however I can.”

Part 2: The FooBaz Bar

The FooBaz Bar is closed. Or at least that’s what the sign on the front of the bright yellow building says. But someone does appear to be inside and the front door is unlocked.
“Hello?”
You can see the bar is covered in a mess of wires and cables.
“Hello?”
The bartender pops up suddenly, looking tired and surprised.
“We’re not open! I’m sorry. We’re dealing with an issue and the drink orders aren’t…”
You interrupt. “That’s why I’m here. I’m helping the Operating Office track down the issues. What’s wrong with the drink orders?”
“Oh,” they respond. “Let me show you!”
A quick cleanup of the wires reveals screens along the top of the bar. “You select the drink you want from this screen.” They tap the beer selection. “And then…”
You notice a thin conveyor belt along the length of the bar. It makes a low, light sound as it moves along the bar and then stops.
The bartender sighs. “You see it’s supposed to deliver your drink.”
They tap the screen a few more times. “We’re storing the drink order here in this array. But when it iterates through the array, it seems to be empty.”

JavaScript: All the Parts pg 132

The spread operator allows an iterable to expand in places where 0 or more elements are expected.


            [...iterable]

            const a = [1, 2, 3];
            const b = [4, 5, 6];
            
            const numbers = [...a, ...b];
            // [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]
            
            const letters = [...'abc', ...'def'];
            // ['a', 'b', 'c', 'd', 'e', 'f']
JavaScript: All the Parts pg 170

In order to be iterable, an object must implement the iterator method, meaning that the object (or one of the objects up its prototype chain) must have a property with a iterator key available via constant Symbol.iterator.


            const iterable = {
              [Symbol.iterator]: function() {
                // Returns an iterator
              },
            };
JavaScript: All the Parts pg 171

Whenever an object needs to be iterated (such as at the beginning of a for..of loop or when paired with the spread operator), its iterator method is called, and the returned iterator is used to obtain the values to be iterated.


            // iterable[Symbol.iterator]()

            for (variable of iterable) {
              statement
            }
